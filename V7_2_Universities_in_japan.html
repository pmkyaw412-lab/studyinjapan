Step 1 — Know the System

National, public or private?

Japan has more than 800 universities, and they fall into three groups. The type affects your tuition, how competitive admission is, how many applications you can make, and the kind of campus you'll join. Here's how they compare.

国 Kokuritsu

National Universities

86 universities · funded by the central government

The most prestigious tier, including the seven former imperial universities. Known for research strength, science and engineering, and a strong academic reputation across Japan and abroad.

Advantages
  • Low, standardised tuition (~¥535,800 / year)
  • High prestige and strong employer recognition
  • Excellent research facilities and funding
  • Many MEXT scholarship places are tied here
Things to weigh
  • Highly competitive admission
  • Often only one application allowed per cycle
  • Most teaching is in Japanese
Typical first year: ~¥818,000 (tuition + one-time entrance fee)
公 Kōritsu

Public Universities

~100 universities · run by prefectures or cities

Run by local governments with a regional mission — teacher training, nursing, agritech and local industry. Often smaller, with strong community ties and a few standout internationally-focused campuses.

Advantages
  • Tuition similar to national universities
  • Smaller classes, strong regional focus
  • Less crowded application competition
  • Programs often linked to local industries
Things to weigh
  • One-time entrance fees can be higher
  • Fewer English-taught programs overall
  • Smaller alumni networks than national schools
Typical first year: ~10% above the national benchmark
私 Shiritsu

Private Universities

~620 universities · ~74% of all undergraduates

The largest group by far. Strong in humanities, social sciences and business, often with modern facilities, the biggest international communities, and the widest range of English-taught degrees.

Advantages
  • You can apply to several at once
  • Most English-taught degree programs
  • Modern campuses and student services
  • Large, active international student bodies
Things to weigh
  • Higher tuition (varies a lot by field)
  • Medicine & dentistry can be very expensive
  • Prestige varies widely between institutions
Typical first year: ~¥1.2–1.6M for humanities; more for STEM/medicine
A quick tip on the word shiritsu: 私立 means a private university, while 市立 (also read shiritsu) means a city-run public one. When in doubt, check the kanji.
Step 2 — Know What's Tested

Understanding the entrance requirements

Most international applicants take one of two routes: a Japanese-taught route built around the EJU and JLPT, or an English-taught route built around English tests and school credentials. Here's what each one involves.

EJU — Examination for Japanese University Admission

Japanese-taught route

The EJU is run by JASSO and, for many universities, replaces a separate entrance exam. It is held twice a year (June and November) in Japan and at test cities across Asia. You choose your subjects based on the major you're aiming for — and every subject except the Japanese language test can be taken in English or Japanese.

You cannot take both Science and Japan & the World — they're for different tracks. A typical science applicant takes Japanese + Math Course 2 + two sciences; a typical humanities applicant takes Japanese + Math Course 1 + Japan & the World. High scorers may also qualify for a JASSO Honors Scholarship.

JLPT — Japanese-Language Proficiency Test

Japanese-taught route

The JLPT measures Japanese ability across five levels, from N5 (beginner) to N1 (advanced). It's held twice a year, in July and December. For Japanese-taught undergraduate study, N2 is the common minimum, while competitive universities and faculties often expect N1. Some universities rely on the EJU's Japanese section instead — always check which your target school uses.

English Tests & School Credentials

English-taught route

A growing number of universities offer full degrees taught in English. For these English-track programs you usually don't need the EJU or JLPT — instead you prove your English level and submit academic credentials from high school.

English proficiency TOEFL iBT or IELTS are the standard for admission. TOEIC is accepted by some programs and is widely used for scholarships and employment.
Academic credentials Many English-track programs ask for SAT, IB, A-Levels or your national exam results, plus transcripts and recommendation letters.
Even on an English-taught degree, learning Japanese makes daily life, part-time work and job hunting far easier. Many students arrive on the English track and graduate fluent.

Another Way In

Professional training colleges (専門学校)

Not every path to a career in Japan runs through a four-year university. 専門学校 (senmon gakkō) are professional training colleges offering focused, hands-on programs — usually two to four years — in fields like IT, games, anime, fashion, music, beauty, cooking, sport, aviation and architecture. Many actively welcome international students and feed directly into industry. They're a strong option in their own right, and a practical alternative if a university place doesn't work out.

Most 専門学校 ask for the JLPT (usually N2) or completion of an approved Japanese language school, rather than the EJU. Entry is generally less competitive than top universities, but graduating qualifies you for a specialist diploma (専門士) and a work visa in your field. Always confirm each school's current requirements directly.

Money Matters

What it costs — and who helps pay

One of the biggest questions for any international student is whether Japan is affordable. The short answer: national universities are surprisingly inexpensive, and several scholarships can cover much of the rest. Here's an honest overview. All figures are approximate and change over time, so always confirm on official pages.

Yearly tuition, by university type

National
¥535,800/ year

A standard government-set rate that's the same at most national universities, plus a one-time admission fee of about ¥282,000 — still the most affordable route. Note that a few top national universities have recently begun charging more: the University of Tokyo raised its undergraduate tuition to about ¥642,960 from 2025, and others may follow.

Public
~¥665,000/ year

Run by local governments. Similar to national tuition, though students from outside the local area often pay a higher admission fee.

Private
¥0.8–1.8M/ year

Varies a lot by faculty: roughly ¥800,000–1,200,000 for humanities, ¥1,200,000–1,800,000 for science and engineering. Medicine and dentistry cost far more.

Monthly living costs

JASSO's national survey puts average student spending at around ¥170,000 per month, with a practical range of ¥130,000–200,000 including rent. Rent is the biggest variable: about ¥41,000 on average nationwide but closer to ¥57,000 in Tokyo. Cities like Fukuoka, Sapporo and regional areas are noticeably cheaper than Tokyo. International students may work up to 28 hours per week (40 during long holidays) to help cover costs.

Major scholarships

MEXT (Monbukagakusho)

Full tuition + ~¥117,000–145,000/month

The Japanese government's flagship scholarship — the most generous available. Covers tuition, a monthly stipend and often airfare. Highly competitive; apply through a Japanese embassy (embassy-recommended) or directly via a university (university-recommended).

Official MEXT info ↗

JASSO Honors Scholarship

¥48,000/month (¥30,000 for language schools)

For privately-financed students already enrolled in Japan with strong academics. A monthly stipend (not tuition) awarded for 6 or 12 months. Applied for through your school after admission.

Official JASSO info ↗

University & private scholarships

Partial to full tuition waivers

Many universities offer their own tuition reductions (often 30–100%) and merit awards for international students. Private foundations (e.g. Rotary Yoneyama) also fund students. Check each university's international admissions page.

Plan Ahead

Application timeline at a glance

Most students aim for the April intake (Japan's main academic year starts in April). Here's a rough month-by-month plan working backwards from there. Dates shift slightly each year, so treat this as a planning guide and confirm exact dates with JASSO and each university.

0 of 5 steps done
~18–12 months before

Research & prepare

Shortlist universities, decide Japanese- vs English-taught, and start studying for the EJU/JLPT or English tests. Begin saving documents (transcripts, certificates).

June & November

Sit the EJU

The EJU runs twice a year — the 1st session in June and the 2nd in November. Many students take both to maximise their score. Applications open roughly February–March (June) and July (November). Results come about 5–6 weeks later.

~Autumn–Winter

Apply to universities

Undergraduate application windows for the April intake typically fall between roughly autumn and winter, varying by university. Submit documents, EJU scores, and sometimes sit an interview or written test.

~Winter–Early Spring

Results & Certificate of Eligibility

Receive admission results, accept your offer and pay fees. Your university helps you apply for the Certificate of Eligibility (COE) — the key document for a student visa.

~Spring

Visa & arrival

Take your COE to a Japanese embassy to get your student visa, arrange housing, and travel to Japan in time for the April start. (Some programs also have a September/October intake.)

Always verify current EJU dates and each university's application deadlines on the JASSO and official university websites — they're set fresh each year.
Common Questions

Questions students ask first

Short, honest answers to the things almost everyone wonders before applying. When a detail can change year to year, confirm it on the official pages linked throughout this guide.

Do I need to speak Japanese to study at a Japanese university?

Not always. A growing number of universities offer full degrees taught in English — for those you prove your English with TOEFL or IELTS and submit your high-school credentials, with no EJU or JLPT required. For Japanese-taught degrees you'll need the EJU and usually the JLPT, where N2 is a common minimum and N1 is expected by competitive faculties. Either way, learning Japanese makes daily life, part-time work and job hunting much easier.

What's the difference between the EJU and the JLPT?

The JLPT only measures your Japanese language ability, across five levels from N5 to N1. The EJU is a university-admissions exam run by JASSO that tests Japanese plus academic subjects — mathematics, science, or Japan & the World — and for many universities it replaces a separate entrance exam. Some schools require both; others accept the EJU's Japanese section in place of the JLPT. Always confirm what your target university asks for.

Do I have to take the EJU in Japanese?

It really depends on the university and program you're aiming for. The EJU itself lets you sit every subject except the Japanese language test in either English or Japanese, and you choose when you register. But each university decides which version it will accept — many Japanese-taught programs expect you to have taken the subjects in Japanese, while English-taught programs are more likely to accept the English version. Always check the language requirement on your target program's admissions page before you register.

How much does it cost to study in Japan?

National universities are the most affordable, with a standard tuition of about ¥535,800 per year — though a few top national universities have recently begun charging more (the University of Tokyo now charges about ¥642,960). Private universities range from roughly ¥0.8M–1.8M depending on the faculty. JASSO's surveys put average living costs around ¥170,000 per month including rent, and regional cities are noticeably cheaper than Tokyo. Scholarships like MEXT and JASSO can cover much of this — see the Costs & Scholarships section above.

Can international students work part-time?

Yes. With permission to engage in an activity other than that permitted under your status of residence, students may work up to 28 hours per week during term, and up to 40 hours per week (8 hours a day) during long university holidays. Many students use part-time work to help cover living costs.

When does the Japanese academic year start?

Most undergraduate programs begin in April, which is why this guide's timeline works backward from an April intake. A growing number of universities also offer a September or October start, especially for English-taught programs.

What is a 専門学校, and is it worth considering?

専門学校 (senmon gakkō) are professional training colleges offering focused, hands-on programs — usually two to four years — in fields like IT, games, anime, design, cooking and hospitality. Entry is generally less competitive than top universities and usually requires the JLPT (often N2) rather than the EJU. Graduating earns a specialist diploma (専門士) and qualifies you for a work visa in your field, so it's a strong path in its own right. There's a full directory in the 専門学校 section above.

How should I prepare for the EJU?

Start 12–18 months ahead. Decide your route and target subjects early (the subject guide in the Requirements section is a good starting point), then build a steady study routine — many students sit the EJU in both June and November to maximise their score.
